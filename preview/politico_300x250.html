Obamacare repeal's exception for Congress stokes charges of hypocrisy

By Jennifer Haberkorn

04/26/17 02:07 PM EDT

Updated 04/26/17 05:11 PM EDT

2017-04-26T05:11-0400
Share on Facebook Share on Twitter

House Republicans say they exempted themselves from insurance requirements in the latest Obamacare repeal bill to avoid violating arcane Senate budget rules, and pledged they would ultimately agree to be covered, along with other Americans, by whatever health care plan they pass.

But Democrats quickly pounced on the exemption for members, accusing the GOP lawmakers of hypocrisy. The Democratic Congressional Campaign Committee said it would release digital ads highlighting the issue in the districts of 30 potentially vulnerable GOP incumbents.

Story Continued Below

"As proof of the repeal bill’s devastating impact, Republican members of Congress are exempting themselves from the punishment they are willing to inflict on their constituents,” said DCCC spokesman Tyler Law.

The exemption would essentially preserve Obamacare coverage protections for politicians and their staffs while allowing states to opt out of such requirements. It stoked new controversy just as the GOP repeal plan gained the endorsement of the House Freedom Caucus because of White House-backed changes.

The language was added by Republicans on the Senate Budget Committee, according to a House GOP aide, to ensure that the repeal plan isn’t struck down by Democrats’ parliamentary challenges if it advances out of the House. The Senate’s complex rules limit what can be taken up under the expedited procedure Republicans want to use to bypass a filibuster and pass a repeal bill with a simple majority of 51 votes.

Trump attacks judiciary for blocking order on sanctuary cities

By Louis Nelson

But a Senate Budget Committee source denied that the committee wrote the provision or even flagged a reconciliation rule problem with the provision.

Republican leadership aides and rank-and-file members insisted the Senate would strike the exemption or that it would be eliminated in separate legislation.

“We understand this is a Byrd rule issue,” Rep. Kevin Cramer (R-N.D.) said after a Republican conference meeting today. “It’s fatal if the House puts in [language that includes members of Congress]. The Senate could put it back.”

Republicans said making Congress comply with the new repeal plan would have involved referring the legislation to a Senate committee that doesn’t have jurisdiction under the expedited process, known as reconciliation. That would have violated the Byrd rule and torpedoed the entire repeal effort, forcing the GOP to come up with 60 votes for a repeal plan. None of the chamber’s 46 Democrats or the two independents aligned with them is expected to break ranks to support a repeal plan.

A spokesperson for Rep. Tom MacArthur (R-N.J.), who helped write the new repeal plan, said the provision will be eliminated.

“Congressman MacArthur does not believe members of Congress or their staff should receive special treatment and is working with House leadership to make absolutely clear that members of Congress and staff are subject to the same rules, provisions and protections as all other Americans,” the spokesperson said.

By Aaron Lorenzo and Colin Wilhelm

Other top Republicans agreed.

“Every member of Congress, I predict, is going to vote to make sure that we’re treated like everyone else,” said Ways and Means Chairman Kevin Brady (R-Texas).

A stand-alone bill could go up for a vote in the House on the same day or in the same vote series as the repeal bill so that lawmakers are confident it would be repaired, according to a Republican aide.

“Of course we’ll all vote for it, and I suspect all Democrats will,” Cramer said.

Obamacare required members of Congress and most of their staff to leave the health plan provided by federal employees and join an Obamacare plan. Many members joined plans on the D.C. exchange so that the federal government would continue to pay for a portion of their plan as their employer. Other members joined their home-state exchange without the federal contribution.

Rachael Bade contributed to this report.
